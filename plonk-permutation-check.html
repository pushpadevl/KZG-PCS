\documentclass{article} \usepackage{amsmath} \usepackage{array} \usepackage{geometry} \geometry{margin=1in} \title{PLONK Grand Product Polynomial Example} \author{Pushpendra Pal} \date{20250601} \begin{document} \maketitle \section*{Overview} This document illustrates a numeric example of the grand product argument in the PLONK zero-knowledge proof protocol. It highlights the role of the random field elements $\beta$ and $\gamma$ in verifying the permutation of wire assignments through a step-by-step computation. \section*{Wire Assignment and Permutation} We consider a simple 2-gate circuit with 6 wire values and a known permutation mapping. \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|} \hline \textbf{Index} & \textbf{Value $v_i$} & \textbf{Permutation $\sigma(i)$} & \textbf{Result Term (mod 17)} \\ \hline 0 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\ 2 & 5 & 3 & 6 \\ 3 & 5 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 3 & 1 & 7 \\ 5 & 8 & 5 & 1 \\ \hline \end{tabular} \section*{Mathematical Steps} We compute the term for each index using the formula: \[ \text{Term}_i = \frac{v_i + \beta \cdot \sigma(i) + \gamma}{v_i + \beta \cdot i + \gamma} \] Let $\beta = 2$, $\gamma = 5$ in the field $\mathbb{F}_{17}$. \[ \text{Term}_0 = \frac{2 + 2 \cdot 0 + 5}{2 + 2 \cdot 0 + 5} = \frac{7}{7} \equiv 1 \mod 17 \] \[ \text{Term}_1 = \frac{3 + 2 \cdot 4 + 5}{3 + 2 \cdot 1 + 5} = \frac{16}{10} \equiv 5 \mod 17 \] \[ \text{Term}_2 = \frac{5 + 2 \cdot 3 + 5}{5 + 2 \cdot 2 + 5} = \frac{16}{14} \equiv 6 \mod 17 \] \[ \text{Term}_3 = \frac{5 + 2 \cdot 2 + 5}{5 + 2 \cdot 3 + 5} = \frac{14}{16} \equiv 3 \mod 17 \] \[ \text{Term}_4 = \frac{3 + 2 \cdot 1 + 5}{3 + 2 \cdot 4 + 5} = \frac{10}{16} \equiv 7 \mod 17 \] \[ \text{Term}_5 = \frac{8 + 2 \cdot 5 + 5}{8 + 2 \cdot 5 + 5} = \frac{6}{6} \equiv 1 \mod 17 \] \section*{Final Verification} Multiplying all terms: \[ 1 \cdot 5 \cdot 6 \cdot 3 \cdot 7 \cdot 1 = 630 \equiv 1 \mod 17 \] This confirms that the grand product argument validates the wire value permutation. \end{document}
